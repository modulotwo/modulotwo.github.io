Complex Numbers

Introduction

With the real numbers (denoted as \(\mathbb{R}\)) we can overcome some limitations posed by dealing only with the rational numbers (\(\mathbb{Q}\)), as when dealing with the equation \(x^2 - 2 = 0\).

But can we come up with an equation having no real solution? Yes. Consider the equation \(x^2 + 2 = 0\). Certainly two positive numbers cannot sum to zero! So we define a new kind of number, which we call complex numbers (\(\mathbb{C}\)).

The complex numbers or imaginary numbers can be added/subtracted and multiplied/divided to produce complex numbers. Such numbers behave nicely as:

  • every real number is a complex number
  • there is a number \(i\) whose square is \(-1\)
  • every complex number can be written as \(a+bi\), with \(a,b\) reals
  • the usual rules of addition and multiplication hold

The distinguishing fact (\(\mathbb{R}\) vs \(\mathbb{C}\)) is that the equation \(x^n = z\) has \(n\) distinct solutions for any nonzero complex number \(z\) and any positive integer \(n\).

Nice thing is: we have multiple representations of a complex number \(z\):

  1. as an ordered pair: \(z = (a,b)\)
  2. in algebraic form: \(z = a + bi\)
  3. in trigonometric form: \(z = |z|(\cos{\theta} + i\sin{\theta})\)
  4. in polar form: \(z = |z|e^{i\theta}\)

Definitions

Ordered pairs

The collection of ordered pairs \((a,b)\) with \(a,b \in \mathbb{R}\) is called the set of complex numbers with the operations of addition and multiplication defined as follows:

$$(a,b) + (c,d) = (a+c, b+d) ~~and~~ (a,b) \cdot (c,d) = (ac-bd, ad+bc)$$

And yes, these numbers behave like real numbers even though they remind you of points in the plane. Addition? Add complex numbers just like you would reals as the usual properties hold (associativity, commutativity, zero, additive inverse).

Subtraction? Just view it as a special case of addition or as the solution to a linear equation that involves addition.

Multiplication? We know from real multiplication that the multiplicative inverse of \(a\) is the number \(b\) such that \(a \cdot b = 1\). So the multiplicative inverse of \((a,b)\) is \(\left(\frac{a}{a^2+b^2}, \frac{-b}{a^2+b^2}\right)\).

Division? Just view it as a special case of multiplication or as the solution of a linear equation that involves multiplication.

Note: all complex numbers of the form \((a,0)\) correspond to the real numbers, so we say that the reals are a subset of the complex numbers.

Note: all complex numbers \((a,b)\) can be uniquely written as a linear combination \(a(1,0) + b(0,1)\). The number \((1,0)\) corresponds to the real number \(1\), but what is the meaning of \((0,1)\)? If we multiply this number by itself, its square yields the real number \(-1\). So we say that \((0,1)\) is the square root of \(-1\), which we denote by \(i\).

Algebraic form

Every complex number can be written uniquely in algebraic form as \(a+bi\) where \(a,b\) are real numbers. The real number \(a\) is called the real part of \(z\) and denoted by \(Re(z)\), and the real number \(b\) is called the *imaginary part\( of $z\) and denoted by \(Im(z)\).

The modulus of a complex number \(z=a+bi\) as \(|z| = \sqrt{a^2+b^2}\), gives the distance from the origin to \((a,b)\).

Given the complex number \(z = a+bi\), we define the complex number \(a-bi\) as its conjugate which we denote \(\bar{z}\).

Note that \(|z| = |\bar{z}|\). Why?

Note that \(|z|^2 = z\bar{z}\), and the modulus of a complex number satisfies the triangle inequality i.e. \(|z+w| \le |z| + |w|\).
