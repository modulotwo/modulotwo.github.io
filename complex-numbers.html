"Complex Numbers"

Introduction

The real numbers (denoted as $\mathbb{R}$) enable us to overcome certain limitations posed by dealing only with the rational numbers ($\mathbb{Q}$), as certain equations which do not have any rational solution have a real solution. For example, consider the equation $x^2 - 2 = 0$. Can we come up with an equation having no real solution? Sure, we can. Consider the equation $x^2 + 2 = 0$. Certainly no two positive numbers can sum to zero! Such equations require that we define a new kind of number, which we will call complex numbers ($\mathbb{C}$).

The complex numbers or imaginary numbers can be added/subtracted and multiplied/divided to produce complex numbers. Such numbers behave nicely as:

  • every real number is a complex number
  • there is a special number $i$ whose square is $-1$
  • every complex number can be represented as $a+bi$, with $a,b$ reals
  • the usual rules of addition and multiplication hold

The distinguishing fact (to distinguish between $\mathbb{R}$ and $\mathbb{C}$) is that the equation $x^n = z$ has $n$ distinct solutions for any nonzero complex number $z$ and any positive integer $n$.

Now we will see a more precise definition and multiple representations of a number $z \in \mathbb{C}$:

  1. as an ordered pair: $z = (a,b)$
  2. in algebraic form: $z = a + bi$
  3. in trigonometric form: $z = |z|(\cos{\theta} + i\sin{\theta})$
  4. in polar form: $z = |z|e^{i\theta}$

Definitions

  1. Ordered pairs

The collection of ordered pairs $(a,b)$ with $a,b \in \mathbb{R}$ is called the set of complex numbers with the operations of addition and multiplication defined as follows: $$(a,b) + (c,d) = (a+c, b+d) ~~and~~ (a,b) \cdot (c,d) = (ac-bd, ad+bc)$$

Wait a minute, ordered pairs of real numbers are numbers? Last I checked ordered pairs meant points in the plane. I'm not sure about this.

Do these numbers really behave like real numbers? Yes, in fact complex numbers behave even better than the reals. First we'll say that complex numbers have the same properties of addition (associativity, commutativity, zero, additive inverse) as the reals.

But what do we make of subtraction? Well, we view it as a special case of addition or as the solution to a linear equation that involves addition.

Next, we look at multiplication. We know from real multiplication that the multiplicative inverse of $a$ is the number $b$ such that $a \cdot b = 1$. With complex numbers as pairs, we have that the multiplicative inverse of $(a,b)$ is $\left(\frac{a}{a^2+b^2}, \frac{-b}{a^2+b^2}\right)$.

And we view division as a special case of multiplication or as the solution of a linear equation that involves multiplication.

Note: all complex numbers of the form $(a,0)$ correspond to the real numbers, so we say that the reals are a subset of the complex numbers.

Note: all complex numbers $(a,b)$ can be uniquely written as $a(1,0) + b(0,1)$, as a linear combination of the complex numbers $(1,0)$ and $(0,1)$. The number $(1,0)$ corresponds to the real number $1$, but what is the meaning of $(0,1)$? If we multiply this number by itself, its square yields the real number $-1$. This allows us to say that $(0,1)$ is the square root of $-1$, which we denote by $i$.

  1. Algebraic form

Every number $z \in \mathbb{C}$ can be written uniquely as a linear combination of $(1,0)$ and $(0,1)$, so it can be written uniquely in algebraic form as $a+bi$ where $a,b$ are real numbers. The real number $a$ is called the real part of $z$ and denoted by $Re(z)$, and the real number $b$ is called the *imaginary part$ of $z$ and denoted by $Im(z)$.

We define the modulus of a complex number $z=a+bi$ as $|z| = \sqrt{a^2+b^2}$, which geometrically refers to the distance from the origin to $(a,b)$.

Given the complex number $z = a+bi$, we define the complex number $a-bi$ as its conjugate which we denote $\bar{z}$.

Note that $|z| = |\bar{z}|$ since the distance from $(0,0)$ to $(a,b)$ is equal to the distance from $(0,0)$ to $(a,-b)$.

Also note that $|z|^2 = z\bar{z}$, and the modulus of a complex number satisfies the triangle inequality i.e. $|z+w| \le |z| + |w|$.
